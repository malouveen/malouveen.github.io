Project Overview

Multicriteria analysis (MCA) is a decision-making tool that allows for the evaluation of multiple criteria in order to make a decision. In this assignment, I will introducte the concept and apply it to present a workflow for determining the suitability of locations for floating solar parks in the Netherlands.

Theoretical Background

What is multicriteria analysis?

Multicriteria analysis (MCA) is a decision-making framework suited for solving problems in which multiple, often conflicting, criteria need to be considered. The general steps taken in this systematic approach are illustrated in figure 1 (Collidu, n.d.)

Consider the example of buying a new car. The future buyer could apply a MCA to make this important decision. Within the first step of the MCA process, the current situation, key players and stakeholder in the decision making process are determined. The key player in our example is the buyer. Next, we identify the various alternative solutions to the problem at hand. Options for the buyer are different types of cars (Skoda Yeti, Suzuki swift, Volkswagen Polo, etc.). The third step is to define the different criteria. A criteria is a clear measure to compare the effects or value of an option. For the buyer the criteria may be price, fuel efficiency, environmental impact, or brand reputation. After this, each criteria is weighed to reflect their relative importance. This weight indicates the difference between options and how much that difference matters. If all the alternatives differ in price by 50 euros, the ‘ price’ criterion will not be weighed heavily, it will be of low importance. In the fifth step the weights are averaged, resulting in the values for each option. Each option is then evaluated based on these values, resulting in a ranking. The best option will have the highest total value (Government Analysis Function, 2023) (Janse, 2026).

Value of MCA in GIS

In the context of geoscience, problems or decisions include many different variables or aspects that need to be considered, so MCA can be used to rank different solutions! GIS often deals with different layers of spatial data such as land use, soil type, or elevation. This makes MCA valuable in GIS, as it allows the integration of multiple spatial factors and criteria to identify optimal locations or solutions for planning, resource management, and decision-making. Thus, MCA makes it possible to go from bulks of raw data to a supported policy decision (Elsevier, n.d.).

Types of questions answered by MCA in GIS can concern the suitability of locations for specific land uses, the prioritization of areas for conservation or development, or the assessment of environmental impacts. Examples include:

  • Where is the best location for a new facility, given environmental, economic, and social criteria? (suitability analysis)
  • Which areas are most suitable for conservation, agriculture, or urban development?

MCA workflow design

The problem

As cities and countries transition to renewable energy, solar power is becoming increasingly important. However, large solar farms often compete with agriculture, housing, and natural habitats for land. To contribute to the energy transition, floating solar parks can be installed on bodies of water, such as lakes and reservoirs. This way, land use conflicts can be avoided. However, not all bodies of water are suitable for floating solar parks, due to environmental, physical, and economic impacts and considerations. Stakeholders in the energy transition, such as the nationa government, water autorities, grid operators, environmental organizations, and local communities each have different priorities wich need to be balences in order to make complex decisions. The decision of where to instal such floating solar parks is complex, as the different objectives and criteria often conflix and need to be weighed against each other. For example, a technically ideal location may be rejected by the local community due to its recreational value. Thus, the goal of this MCA is to identify the most suitable locations for floating solar parks in the Netherlands, taking into account various criteria that affect their feasibility and impact (World Bank Group et al., 2019).

The MCA approach

The aim of this Multi-Criteria Analysis (MCA) is to answer the following question:

Which bodies of water in the Netherlands are most suitable for floating solar parks?

The suitability of a body of water for floating solar parks depends on multiple factors. For this analysis, I consider various criteria which are relevant from a physical, environmental, and economical perspective. The following criteria are considered, and their contribution to the suitability is noted.

  • Water depth: moderate depths are most suitable for anchoring and maintance.
  • Surface area: large bodies are suitable for generating large amounts of electricity
  • Water body type: closed bodies are suitable for stable conditions
  • Water flow velocity: low flow velocity is suitable for low reducing stress on the floating installation
  • Distance to grid connection: locations close to existing grid connections are more suitable
  • Proximity to protected areas: locations far from protected areas are more suitable to prevent environmental impacts.
These criteria are important because they each affect the feasibility, efficiency, and environmental impact of floating solar parks. For example, water depth affects the stability and anchoring of the solar panels, while surface area affects the potential energy output. Water body type and flow velocity affect the suitability for installation and maintenance. Distance to grid connection affects the cost and efficiency of transmitting electricity. Proximity to protected areas affects the environmental impact and regulatory compliance. Thus, while each criteria can be attributed to a different field the criteria are interconnected, making this analysis interdisciplinary in nature.

This goes hand in hand with trade-offs, such as surface area (technical) and proximity to protected area (environmental). Large water bodies are favoured for floating solar panels, but usualy such water bodies are located in close proximity to protected areas.

The Data
For this MCA, many datasets would be needed. The following GIS layers would be required:
  • Bathymetry: for assessing water depth (EMODnet Bathymetry Consortium, n.d.)
  • BGT: for assessing the size of the water body (PDOK, n.d.)
  • A dataset for water body type and flow velocity
  • Enexis: for assessing the distance to the nearest grid connection (Enexis Netbeheer, n.d.)
  • NNN: for identifying areas with environmental restrictions (PDOK, n.d.)
The Workflow

The Main Findings

The figure above graphically illustrates the MCA workflow to find suitable locations for floating solar panels in the Netherlands. The criteria are described and which datasets can be used to quantify these. The data sources are managed and provided by a different group of people from a different field of work. This indicates that solving it requires the collaboration of different disciplines. While MCA is a tool which will contribute the decision-making process, it also helps structuring this complex process of interdisciplinary collaboration. Not only does each stakeholder have different values, requirements and preferences, but the data is collected in varying units and standardizations. Besides this, choices need to be made in favour of one criterion while negatively affecting another. These trade-offs and the discussions around them can take a lot of time. All in all, considering different criteria with each their own stakeholders, units, and preferences and combining this into one decision is very challenging, but a multi-criteria analysis creates a structured approach, making the decision process just a little easier.

Reflection

This assignment helped me realise that a multi-criteria analysis is more complex than it seems. When the criteria are presented, the analysis seems quite straightforward. When determining the criteria myself, I realised how many things need to be taken into consideration when it comes to the suitability of a location for a floating solar panel installation. There are criteria that I did not incorporate, such as economic viability, public perception, policy restriction, competing water use, subsidy opportunities, and many more. Note that the tools I mentioned in my workflow are not the only options. ArcGIS could be used instead of QGIS and excel can be replaced by many applications.

Finally, I did not consider the bureaucratic aspect of such an analysis. Many different people need to work together to achieve a decision, which can be very inefficient and frustrating at times. I can already imagine how many meetings will be necessary for each stakeholder to be content with the decision. MCA is a good tool to make the process more objective and data based.
